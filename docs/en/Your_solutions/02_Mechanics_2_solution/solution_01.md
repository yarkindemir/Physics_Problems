## 1. Gravitational Dependence

A simple pendulum has a period of 4 seconds on Earth. What would its period be on the Moon, where the gravitational acceleration is about 1/6 of Earth's?

What is the required length of a simple pendulum to have a period of exactly 1 second on Earth?

---

### Formula

For a simple pendulum:

$$
T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}
$$

Where:

- $T$ = period
- $L$ = length
- $g$ = gravitational acceleration

---

### Part A: Period on the Moon

We know:

- Period on Earth:

$$
T_E = 4\ \text{s}
$$

- Gravity on the Moon:

$$
g_M = \frac{g_E}{6}
$$

Using the pendulum formula, the period is inversely proportional to the square root of gravity:

$$
T_M = T_E \sqrt{\frac{g_E}{g_M}}
$$

Substitute:

$$
T_M = 4 \sqrt{\frac{g_E}{g_E/6}}
$$

$$
T_M = 4\sqrt{6}
$$

$$
T_M \approx 4 \times 2.45
$$

$$
T_M \approx 9.8\ \text{s}
$$

### Answer for Part A:

$$
\boxed{T_M \approx 9.8\ \text{s}}
$$

---

### Part B: Length for a 1-second pendulum on Earth

We use:

$$
T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}
$$

Given:

$$
T = 1\ \text{s}
$$

So:

$$
1 = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}
$$

Divide both sides by $2\pi$:

$$
\frac{1}{2\pi} = \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}
$$

Square both sides:

$$
\frac{1}{4\pi^2} = \frac{L}{g}
$$

Multiply both sides by $g$:

$$
L = \frac{g}{4\pi^2}
$$

Take:

$$
g = 9.81\ \text{m/s}^2
$$

Then:

$$
L = \frac{9.81}{4\pi^2}
$$

$$
L \approx \frac{9.81}{39.48}
$$

$$
L \approx 0.248\ \text{m}
$$

### Answer for Part B:

$$
\boxed{L \approx 0.248\ \text{m}}
$$

---

## Final Answers

- Period on the Moon:

$$
\boxed{9.8\ \text{s}}
$$

- Length for a 1-second pendulum on Earth:

$$
\boxed{0.248\ \text{m}}
$$
